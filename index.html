21st Century Conflict

New-Age Wars

Wars today don't always use guns and bombs. Some wars are silent, digital, economic, or invisible — but they still affect people, children, and the planet. Here are four types of new-age wars.

🦠

Biological Threats

"When health becomes a global concern"

Causes

  • Poor cooperation between countries on health safety
  • Unsafe research or lack of proper rules and regulations
  • Natural disease spread made worse by conflict
  • Lack of equal access to healthcare around the world

Effects

  • On People: Health crises, loss of jobs, fear and isolation
  • On Children: Missing school, mental health challenges, reduced social interaction and learning gaps
  • On Environment: More medical waste, increased use of chemicals, wildlife disturbances

Student takeaway: "Global health needs cooperation, not conflict."

📡

Information War

"Controlling minds instead of land"

Causes

  • Political influence and trying to control what people think
  • Spreading fear or hatred through fake news and social media
  • Winning people's support without actually fighting
  • Disagreements between countries over territory or power

Effects

  • On People: Confusion and mistrust, division in society, fear, anger and prejudice
  • On Children: Difficulty knowing what is true, anxiety due to negative news, forming opinions without knowing all the facts
  • On Environment: Environmental issues ignored or denied, false information about climate change delays action

Student takeaway: "Truth is powerful — and must be protected."

📦

Trade War

"When countries fight over money and goods"

Causes

  • Economic competition — countries wanting to be richer and more powerful
  • Protecting home industries from foreign competition
  • Control of important resources like technology, oil, and land
  • Unfair trade rules that hurt one country more than another

Effects

  • On People: Job losses, higher prices for everyday goods, reduced income
  • On Children: Families under financial stress, fewer opportunities, reduced access to education or nutrition
  • On Environment: Overuse of natural resources, unsafe mining and manufacturing, increased pollution

Student takeaway: "Economic decisions can hurt people even without violence."

💻

Cyber War

"Battles fought through screens"

Causes

  • Control of important information and government data
  • Spying and data theft between countries
  • Trying to break or damage another country's computer systems
  • Gaining power through technology instead of armies

Effects

  • On People: Banking systems hacked, power cuts and transport failures, loss of privacy and trust
  • On Children: Online safety risks, exposure to fake news, cyberbullying linked to misinformation, increased screen dependency
  • On Environment: High energy use by data centres, e-waste from damaged systems, increased carbon footprint of technology

Student takeaway: "Technology can protect or harm — depending on how we use it."

Traditional War

Before new-age wars, conflicts were fought with soldiers and weapons. Let's explore some of the most important wars in history and what caused them — and what happened after.

Causes of War Why It Starts

  • ›Political Alliances — Countries made agreements to protect each other, which meant one country's war became everyone's war.
  • ›Nationalism — Extreme pride in one's country, sometimes leading to conflicts with others.
  • ›Competition for Power — Countries wanting to be in control and have more influence than others.
  • ›Political Ideology — Disagreements about how a country should be run — democracy vs. communism, for example.
  • ›Economic Crisis — Poverty and inequality made people angry, which leaders sometimes used to start wars.
  • ›Desire for Revenge — Countries that felt mistreated after past wars wanted to take back what they had lost.

Effects of War The Aftermath

  • ›Massive Loss of Life — Millions of soldiers and civilians died in both World Wars.
  • ›Cities Destroyed — Bombs and fighting reduced entire cities to rubble, forcing people to flee.
  • ›Children Affected — Millions of children lost their homes, families, and schools.
  • ›Environmental Damage — Long-term harm to nature, soil, and water from bombs and chemicals used in war.
  • ›Social Change — Wars changed how societies thought about equality, government, and human rights.
  • ›Fear of Nuclear War — After WWII, countries built nuclear weapons, making people fear another devastating conflict.

The Propaganda War (1950s–1990s)

During the Cold War, the USA and the Soviet Union did not fight each other directly. Instead, they tried to control people's thinking using posters, radio, television, movies, and even school textbooks. Each country showed itself as good and peaceful, and the other as dangerous or evil. This is called propaganda.

Children who grew up during this time were often taught to fear the other side. They practiced safety drills at school and grew up believing one side was always right. This shows us that wars aren't just fought with weapons — they can also be fought with words and ideas.

"In war, there are no unwounded soldiers."

A Path Forward

Preventing War

Peace doesn’t just happen on its own — it takes effort, understanding, and cooperation. Here are some ways that people and countries can work together to prevent war.

"Peace cannot be kept by force; it can only be achieved by understanding."
— Albert Einstein
01

Talking It Out

When countries have disagreements, they can talk to each other through embassies and international meetings. This helps solve problems before they turn into conflicts. Good communication can prevent many wars.

02

Education

Learning about history, other cultures, and human rights helps young people understand the world better. When we understand each other, we are less likely to fear or fight each other. Education is one of the most powerful tools for peace.

03

International Rules

Organisations like the United Nations help countries follow rules and settle disagreements fairly. When countries respect these rules, it becomes harder for conflicts to start.

04

Trading Together

When countries buy and sell things from each other, they become partners. This makes war more costly and less attractive, because hurting the other country would also hurt yourself.

05

Reducing Inequality

Many conflicts start because some people have much more than others. When everyone has access to food, healthcare, and education, there is less anger and resentment that can lead to war.

06

Learning About Each Other

When people learn about different cultures, traditions, and ways of life, they replace fear with respect. Exchange programmes, art, and shared stories help build connections between people from different countries.

07

Youth Action

Young people like us can make a difference! Students around the world have helped change laws and start important conversations about peace. Every voice matters — including yours.

08

Stopping the Spread of Fake News

False information can make people afraid and angry, and can even push countries toward war. When we check facts before sharing them, we help protect the truth — and protect peace.

09

Global Cooperation on Health

Diseases don’t respect borders. When countries work together on health challenges — sharing medicine, research, and resources — they help each other instead of competing. Cooperation saves lives.

What Can You Do?

Peace doesn't only happen between governments. It starts with the small choices you make every day — choosing kindness over cruelty, curiosity over prejudice, and conversation over conflict. You don't have to be a world leader to make a difference.

Be informed. Be kind. Be the change.

The Team

About Us

We are a group of five students - Jaish, Anup, Aryan, Hassan and Ayansh - who believe that world peace can only be achieved from collective efforts.

Who We Are

We are five students in the IB Primary Years Programme. We come from different backgrounds, but we share one strong belief: that war causes too much suffering, and that it is our job as young people to say — enough. The future belongs to us, and we choose peace.

Our Mission

Our PYPX Exhibition is our way of speaking out for world peace. We say no to all kinds of war — whether it's fought with weapons, computers, fake news, or money. Every part of this website is a message: conflict is a choice, and so is peace. We choose peace.

Our Central Idea

Wars have shaped the past — and they still affect the world today. From old battlefields to modern cyberattacks, conflict is something humans have always faced. But humans are also capable of kindness, forgiveness, and working things out. Understanding why wars happen is the first step to stopping them.

What We Learned

We learned that wars are never simple, and they are rarely unavoidable. They often start because of unfairness, greed, or a breakdown in communication. But we also found something hopeful — peace is always possible when people choose to talk instead of fight. Conversation, education, and empathy are powerful tools.

Our Inquiry

We explored how wars have changed over time — from World War I to modern cyber and information wars. We looked at how conflicts affect ordinary people: families losing their homes, children missing school, and communities struggling to rebuild. We also studied what helps bring peace, like international cooperation and youth action.

Our Action

This website is our action. We built it not just as a school project, but as a way to share what we learned and inspire others. We believe that when people understand the causes and effects of war, they are more likely to work for peace. If this website makes even one person think differently, our effort was worth it.
